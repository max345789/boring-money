How much does a car wash actually make?

The car wash is one of the most misunderstood businesses in the world. From outside, it looks like a commodity — a shed with brushes and soap. From inside, it's a cash flow machine.

The Business Model

The modern car wash looks nothing like the shed-and-bucket operation most people imagine. Today's automated tunnel washes process 150–300 cars per day on a single lane. The variable cost per car? $1.50 to $3.00. That's soap, water, electricity, and chemical solution. Everything else is fixed cost — the building, the equipment, the land.

What makes the car wash fascinating is the subscription model. Operators report 60–70% of total revenue from monthly unlimited wash plans priced at $20–$40 per month. The average subscriber washes 3.2 times per month. Do the math — the operator pays $6–$10 in variable costs and collects $25–$40. That's SaaS margins in a concrete box.

The Operator: Dennis in Fresno

Dennis owns four car wash locations across Fresno, California. He works approximately 20 hours per week, primarily on financial oversight and staffing. His four locations combined generate roughly $500k in monthly revenue and net him approximately $340,000 annually after all expenses, debt service, and taxes.

Dennis didn't start with millions. He bought his first wash — a struggling self-serve location — for $180,000 using an SBA loan. He converted it to an express exterior tunnel over 18 months, investing an additional $2.1M in equipment and construction. That single location now generates $45k in monthly net profit.

The Risks

Car washes are capital-intensive. A new tunnel build costs $3M–$5M depending on real estate. Equipment breaks — and when it breaks, your revenue stops instantly. Weather dependency is real in northern climates. And increasingly, private equity roll-ups are entering markets and competing on price, making independent operation harder.
